Before you write a single line of a pitch deck, spend five minutes on the partner's portfolio page. It's the most honest document a VC produces — more honest than their website copy, more honest than their podcast. Here's what to actually look for.

## 1. Check size, inferred from round stage

You won't always see cheque sizes, but you can infer them from the *stage* at which this partner's name shows up. If they're consistently the named partner on seed rounds with two co-founders and no product, that's your stage. If every deal you can find is Series B onward, a seed-stage pitch to this partner is probably a calendar-filler, not a real opportunity — however warm the intro felt.

## 2. Sector clustering — and what's *missing*

Look for the cluster, but also look for the gap. A partner with eight fintech infrastructure investments and zero consumer fintech bets has almost certainly formed a thesis about *why* consumer fintech doesn't work for them — even if their bio says "fintech." If your company is consumer fintech, that gap is a signal worth addressing head-on in your outreach, not ignoring.

## 3. Co-investor patterns

Who else shows up across multiple deals with this partner? Recurring co-investors tell you who this partner trusts, trades information with, and might already be talking to about your space. If you can find a warm path through one of those co-investors instead of a cold inbound, take it — it's frequently a faster route to a first meeting than the firm's own contact form.

## 4. The shape of the exits (and the write-offs)

A portfolio full of fast Series A→B→C markups but few exits tells you this is a firm optimised for paper markups and follow-on rounds — useful to know if you're raising a "story round." A portfolio with a few real exits and a long tail of quiet companies tells you this partner is patient and has probably seen a multi-year journey before. Neither is wrong, but they want different things from *you* in a first meeting.

## 5. How recently did they do *anything* in your space?

If the most recent investment that looks like yours is three years old, either the thesis has gone cold, or the partner has simply been busy elsewhere. Either way, don't assume the old investment is still "their thing" — ask, gently, what's changed in how they think about that space since.

Five minutes on a portfolio page won't tell you everything. But it will tell you whether the meeting you're about to have is the one you think it is.
