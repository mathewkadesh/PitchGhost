"Too early" is the most common pass a founder hears, and the least understood. Founders interpret it as a timeline problem — come back in six months with more revenue — and sometimes that's exactly right. But more often, "too early" is shorthand for something more specific: **the partner doesn't yet have the evidence they'd need to convince their partnership.**

## It's an internal-memo problem, not a you-problem

Every partner who wants to invest has to go and sell the deal internally — to an investment committee, to other GPs, sometimes to an LP advisory board. "Too early" frequently means: *I believe you, but I can't yet write the memo that gets this through committee.*

That reframes the question you should be asking. Not "how do we get bigger," but **"what's the one data point that would let this partner make the internal case?"** For some firms that's a logo. For others it's a retention curve. For a few, it's simply a second founder who de-risks the team.

## Three flavours of "too early"

1. **Category too early** — the partner agrees the problem is real but doesn't believe the market is investable yet (regulation, infrastructure, or buyer behaviour hasn't caught up). No amount of your traction fixes this. Ask directly: "what would need to be true about the market, not the company, for this to be fundable?"
2. **Evidence too early** — the idea and market are fine, but the proof points the firm needs (often: a repeatable sales motion, a cohort that doesn't churn, or a second hire who validates the team) don't exist yet. This is the most common, and the most fixable.
3. **Relationship too early** — the partner likes the space and the founder, but wants to watch you operate for a quarter or two before committing. This often shows up as "let's stay in touch" — which, frustratingly, can be sincere.

## What to do with it

Don't argue with "too early." Instead, ask the partner to be specific: *"If we come back in [N] months, what's the single thing you'd want to see different?"* The answer tells you which of the three flavours you're dealing with — and whether this firm is worth your time on the next round at all.
