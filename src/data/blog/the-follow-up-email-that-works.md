Most post-pitch emails are a thank-you note with a deck attached. They get filed, politely, into the part of an investor's inbox that never gets a reply. The emails that get a second meeting do three things differently — and none of them are about being more grateful.

## 1. They answer the unasked question

In almost every first meeting, there's a moment where the partner's face changes — a pause before a question, a "interesting, and how do you think about…" that trails off. That's the real objection. Your follow-up should open by addressing *that*, directly, before anything else: *"You asked about [X] — I've thought about it more and here's the sharper answer…"* This signals you were listening, not performing.

## 2. They add one new fact, not a recap

Don't summarise the deck again. Add exactly one thing that wasn't in the room: a number that moved since the meeting, a customer reference you can now share, a hire you closed. New information gives the partner a reason to re-open the thread rather than file it.

## 3. They mirror the firm's own language

If the partner's public writing talks about "category-defining" or "platform risk" or "founder-market fit," use *their* vocabulary, not generic VC-speak. It's a small thing, but it reads as "this founder gets how we think" — which is exactly the signal a partner needs to bring a deal to their partnership.

## A structure that works

- **Line 1:** Direct answer to the open question from the meeting.
- **Line 2–3:** One new, concrete data point.
- **Line 4:** A specific, low-friction next step — not "let me know if you'd like to continue," but "we're closing this data room Friday — happy to get [specific person] 20 minutes with your team before then if useful."

Keep it under 150 words. The goal isn't to re-pitch — it's to give the partner a reason to act *this week*, while the meeting is still fresh in their mind.
